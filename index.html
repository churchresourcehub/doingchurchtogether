The Toolbox

The Church Resource Hub

A shared library of systems, processes, and working documents from real churches, nearly 150 resources across every ministry area: orientation packets and budgets, event playbooks and safety policies, frameworks and worship plans. Nothing theoretical, nothing gated; every card is a document a congregation actually uses, offered so yours can adapt it instead of starting from a blank page.

How to use it: search for what you need or browse by ministry area, download the file, and make it your own. Stars mark the cornerstone documents, and the contribute page lets your church add its best work to the library.

How it connects: every "Grab the tools" list in the Pastor's Handbook points to a live card here. The book tells you what the tool is for; the hub hands it to you.

The Map

The Pastor's Handbook

A digital field guide for leading a local church, written for first-time and newly appointed pastors, Course of Study students, and workshop cohorts. Thirty-four chapters across eight parts walk the whole job, from the pastor's own call and inner life through worship, care, discipleship, missions, money, and leading change, each chapter teaching one piece of the work in plain language, telling a true story of how it went at a real church, and ending with concrete steps.

How to use it: read it straight through or jump to the chapter for the season you are in, take its one next step this week, and follow its links to the exact documents you need.

How it connects: the handbook is the map to the hub's toolbox. Where the hub gives you the document, the book gives you the path, and its running case study is the same congregation whose files fill the library.

The Thinking

Woods Lisenby: Theology and Practice

A connected systematic of twenty-three doctrines drawn from one pastor's written and preached words across seventeen years of ministry, mapped so that every doctrine hangs by a visible line from the foundation it depends on, alongside the sermons themselves and the practice library his church runs on. An assistant answers your questions from the full text of all of it, anchored in what was actually written and preached rather than in the internet's guesses.

How to use it: click any doctrine to read its full treatment, in the scholar's version or the plain-language one, and follow the connections from one locus into the next, or simply ask the assistant a question, about grace, about Scripture, about women in ministry, and read the sources it answers from.

How it connects: the practice in the hub and the handbook stands on this thinking. If the book ever makes you ask why behind a how, this is where the why lives.

The Compass

Comparative Theology

A companion to the theology that sets one pastor's Wesleyan convictions inside the wider family of Christian traditions. Compare what the churches believe, doctrine by doctrine, and trace how they branched across two thousand years, from the early church to the many streams alive today. Every tradition is drawn fairly from its own voice, so the differences stay honest and the common ground stays real.

How to use it: pick any two questions and watch the traditions sort themselves across the map, read a single doctrine straight across the families, or open a tradition to meet its founders and the authors you would find on the shelf today. A built-in assistant compares any two traditions for you and answers from the sources.

How it connects: the Theology gives you Woods's own systematic, and this sets that systematic in the wider landscape. You can see where your own congregation stands, and understand the neighbors you share a town and a mission with.
